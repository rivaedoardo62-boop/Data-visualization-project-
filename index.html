The Italian Stagnation

Real Wages Have Stood Still While the Rest of Western Europe Has Moved On

Across Western Europe, real wages have grown by anywhere from 5 to more than 60 percent over the last three decades. In Italy, the figure is barely 3.

An Investigation, Data Visualizations 2026
By Filippo Nannucci · Daniele Giovanardi · Edoardo Riva · Published May 12, 2026

In 1995, the average Italian worker earned, in dollars adjusted for purchasing power, slightly less than a German colleague and just a little more than a Swedish one. Three decades later, the two northern peers have pulled away by tens of percentage points, while the Italian wage in real terms is essentially the same as it was thirty years ago. It is, by all available measures, a singular case in Western Europe.

The figures come from the OECD’s Average Annual Wages database, which tracks compensation in constant U.S. dollars at purchasing-power parity. The chart that opens this story is built from that series, with each country indexed to its starting value. The red line is Italy. The greys are its peers.

When the Divergence Began

The divergence is not gradual. Through the first decade, through the dot-com bust, through the early-2000s slowdown, the Italian line moves in step with Germany’s, occasionally even ahead of it. After 2008 it breaks. The Italian wage peaks in 2010 and then begins falling. By 2024 it is essentially back where it started thirty years ago, while Germany has gained roughly twenty index points, France twenty-five, and Sweden more than sixty.

“Italian wages are not just low. They are, on average, the same as they were in 1995, while peers’ wages have risen by up to sixty percent.”

A Wall of Red

Is the Italian stagnation a national fact or a southern one? When Italians talk about the country’s economic problems, the conversation almost always lands in the same place: il Mezzogiorno. The South pulls down the average; the North (Lombardia, Veneto, Emilia-Romagna) keeps Italy on the European map. The popular picture is of a country split in half: an industrial engine at the top, a slower periphery at the bottom. If that picture is right, a regional map of growth between 2000 and 2024 should reveal exactly that: green at the top, red at the bottom.

It does not. Once the eye moves past the colour, the popular north-south frame collapses entirely. The slowest Italian region between 2000 and 2024 is not in the Mezzogiorno but in the centre: Umbria, with growth of just 56 percent against the Eurozone’s 99. Lazio, Molise, Trento and Emilia-Romagna sit immediately behind it. Lombardia, the engine of the Italian economy, has grown twenty-six percentage points more slowly than the Euro area. The only region that essentially matches the European pace is Bolzano, an alpine outlier in the country’s far north. The stagnation is, with that lone exception, everywhere. And it is in places no Italian narrative would have predicted.

Why the Engine Stopped

“Si lavora di più e si guadagna di meno” (“we work more and earn less”), is one of the most quoted lines of contemporary Italian self-criticism, heard at family dinners, in newspaper editorials, on television. Its scientific cousin in policy debate has a name: nanismo, the country’s chronic firm smallness. Italian firms, the argument runs, are too small to invest, too small to specialise, too small to compete on global markets. But the diagnosis bundles two distinct claims usually heard together: that Italy has more small firms than its peers, and that Italian small firms produce less than their European counterparts. Are both true, or only one?

Both. Italian micro-firms (those with fewer than ten employees) employ 42 percent of the country’s workforce, twice the share of Germany (19 percent) or France (23 percent). And those Italian micro-firms produce only 28 thousand euros per worker, against 45 thousand in Germany and 46 thousand in France. The two effects compound: Italy is unusually concentrated in the size class that produces the least, and that size class itself produces less in Italy than abroad. The Coxcomb makes the doubling visible at a glance: Italy’s red core is both the deepest red and the largest, while Germany’s green outer rim, its very-large firms producing 76 thousand per worker, has no Italian equivalent. The structural diagnosis is right.

Italy Is Frozen, but for Everyone?

So far we have looked at the country, the regions, the firms. The last question is generational. The popular intuition in Italy is that the young pay the price of the long stagnation, while older workers (sheltered by tenure and collective contracts) hold on. The chart below tests that. The intuition is wrong. Italian real hourly wages did decline for every age band, but the band that lost the most was the oldest. Between 2014 and 2023, the under-thirty saw their median wage fall by 9.4 percent, the 30–49 band by 10.8 percent, and the 50+ band by 12.8 percent. The chart also exposes the timing: the 2014 and 2021 columns are nearly identical. Wages were essentially flat for seven years, then collapsed in the two years that followed, as cumulative HICP inflation of 15 percent went unabsorbed by collective-bargaining adjustments. No Italian generation got out cheap.

A Note on Honesty. The Same Data, Two Stories

Beyond region, firm size and age, there is a fifth lens often invoked in the Italian wage debate: taxation. Workers are routinely told that their take-home pay is small because the State takes too much. To anchor that claim, here are two static figures of the same OECD tax-wedge dataset, designed to push opposite editorial readings. The first follows our usual standards. The second uses four subtle manipulation levers (cherry-picked comparators, a non-standard income level, a non-standard denominator, and a populist title) to spin the same numbers into a populist anti-State headline. Both figures are mathematically defensible. Only one is honest.

Italy sits at 47.0 percent, fifth out of eighteen, in a tight cluster with Belgium (52.4), Germany (47.9), France (47.1) and Austria (47.1), the European welfare-state group. High, but not anomalous. Now consider the same dataset, told differently.

Both charts use the same OECD database. Both are mathematically valid. The difference between “Italy is high but in line with peers” and “Italy taxes its workers more harshly than anywhere comparable” is the difference between four small editorial choices (comparator selection, income bracket, denominator, headline), none of which are obvious to a reader skimming a newspaper page. The lesson is not that data lies. The lesson is that data does whatever its author wants it to do.

What It Cost You, Personally

Numbers, as the previous pair shows, can be told in many ways. At the end of the month, what matters is what your bank account actually shows. This last visualization hands the keyboard to you: pick the year you started working in Italy and a European country to compare with. The calculator integrates the year-by-year real-wage gap into one cumulative number, then translates it into something concrete (cars, months of Milan rent, Vespas, weeks of vacation, or pizzas).

The numbers in this article are aggregates: nineteen index points behind Germany, sixty behind Sweden, three percent of growth in thirty years. The number on your screen is what those aggregates mean for you. That, in the end, is the only translation that matters.
